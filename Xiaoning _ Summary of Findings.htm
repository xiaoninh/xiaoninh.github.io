♥ Summary of Findings and Interface Enhancements ♥

This page documents of process of buiding my personal portfolio, which includes the process of conducting research and testing, summary of findings from research and testing, and also the adjustments made to incorportate the findings.



User Reserch

User Interviews



Before building my portfolio, in order to gather insights about what people expect to see on a personal portfolio website, I conducted 4 user interviews. The interviewees are peer students at UCI, some of them are in related technology fields some of them are not. The information provided by the interviewees provide insites about what features and functions I should include in my portfolio.


Competitor Analysis



After collecting user options about what they want to see on a portfolio website, in order to understand what kinds of similar websites are out there and what competitors are doing, I conducted a competitor analysis on websites that serve similar purposes

portfolio, an artist’s portfolio, and also the LinkedIn profile pages as my targets. Through viewing their contents and page designs, I analyzed their strengths and weaknesses and formulated ideas about how I want my portfolio to look like.

Feature Value Matrix



After user interviews and competitor Analysis, I have come up with ideas of what features to include in my portfolio. I then rated these features based on their user value, business value, and technical effort in a Feature Value Matrix.


Feature Prioritization



After rating each feature, I prioritized those features based on their ratings, and visualized them on more graphs. Prioritizing these features allow me to focus on the features that providing the great value with little efforts needs first.


User Testing

User Stories



After built up the first version of my personal portfolio, I did 10 user stories in order to generally understand who my users are, what they want, and what they need to accomplish by using my interface. These stories are based on the features currently provided by my portfolio, and describe each type of users’ incentives on using specific features or contents.


Cognitive Walkthrough



To identify usability issues of the current version portfolio, I did cognitive walkthrough with 3 individual users by asking to complete a series of tasks on my interface. By asking user questions along the walkthrough and observe their actions, I obtained feedbacks about the current interface and identified several important weaknesses, such as the choice of background color, plain design, and lack of details, etc,.


User Test



I conducted two user testings by using the tools provided by usability hub. The first test was a click test asking user where would they click if they want to know the previous work, the second test was a preference test comparing two version of page design and asking user to choose the one they think is better. Both test have yield useful results and revealed usability problems that I never have thought before.

Summary of Findings

By conducting a series of cognitive walkthrough and user tastings, I have identified several usability issues of my portfolio. The first issue identified was the pink background color of the page. While myself love pink very much, I did not even hesitate to use nothing else but all pink as my background. However, 2 of my walkthrough users reflected that the pink color was too solid on the background and made the portfolio seem less professional. This problem was also reflected by the second user test when comparing two designs of the page. 13 out of 25 users chose the second page because the first page is full of pink and the second one has it with white.

The second issue found was that the portfolio lack of details and picture demonstrations of certain projects and research. Most users from the cognitive walkthrough said that the descriptions of projects were too brief right now which does not allow them to understand the projects thoroughly, and they wanted to have someplace to click on to have a close look at the project.

The other issue identified was the overall plain design of the portfolio. The only visual elements of the portfolio are the profile picture, and the pink background which has been complained by many. All the other contents are demonstrated poorly in bullet points on the page. Most users from both the walkthrough and testing suggest adding more pictures or other visual design to the portfolio, in order to demonstrate the artistic aspect of a UX designer.

Incorporation of Findings

In order to address the usability issues mentioned above and to improve the overall design and user experience, rather than making changes on the current version, I built a totally new portfolio page on anther GitHub repository. I started with sketching to re-design the lay out and contents of my portfolio. In my new portfolio, these followings points are the major changes I have make to incorporate the findings:

  • I used a combination of pink and white as my background color instead of using all pink to make the website look more soft and official.

  • I moved most of the “about me” contents into a separate "about me" page and only left simple information on the top of home page.

  • Instead of listing everything with bullet points, I organized the projects into different sections, separated with white spacing.

  • There is a “more” button added to each project on the home page, which allow users to click on and to check out more detail information about each project.

  • Finally, In order to make the new portfolio site more visual, I also added pictures to each of the project, and I draw my own button to navigate to the about me page.
